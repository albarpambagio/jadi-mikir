# learning-science.md
## Principles behind this MCQ platform
## Source: *The Math Academy Way* (Skycak, Math Academy 2026)

---

## Why this platform exists

One-on-one tutoring produces outcomes 2 standard deviations above traditional classroom instruction. This platform tries to close that gap — not with a tutor, but with a question engine that adapts to how each learner actually performs.

Everything runs in the browser. No account, no server, no tracking. The learner's state lives on their device.

---

## The 10 Principles

### 1. Active Learning
> Learning = producing answers, not consuming content.

Watching videos and re-reading notes feel productive but produce minimal retention. The only thing that reliably moves knowledge into long-term memory is being forced to retrieve it. Questions aren't a test at the end — they are the learning itself.

**Platform rule:** Questions appear immediately. There is no "read first" mode.

---

### 2. Mastery Learning
> Move forward only when you truly know it.

Getting one question right doesn't mean you know a topic. Mastery means consistent, independent retrieval — defined here as 3 consecutive correct answers, or ≥80% accuracy over the last 5 attempts. The bar is never lowered; more practice is added instead.

**Platform rule:** A topic stays locked until the mastery threshold is met. No skipping.

---

### 3. Spaced Repetition
> Review at the moment you're about to forget.

Memory decays predictably without review. Reviewing at expanding intervals (3 days → 7 → 16 → 35...) restores and strengthens the memory trace more efficiently than any amount of re-reading. Reviews should feel hard — easy reviews mean you're reviewing too soon.

**Platform rule:** Mastered topics re-enter the queue on a schedule. Reviews are served before new learning.

---

### 4. Retrieval Practice
> Being tested IS being taught.

Every time the brain retrieves a piece of information from memory, that memory becomes stronger and longer-lasting. This is true even when the retrieval fails — the effort of trying strengthens the trace. Frequent low-stakes quizzes both improve performance and reduce test anxiety.

**Platform rule:** The question queue is the primary learning mechanism, not a checkpoint.

---

### 5. Desirable Difficulty
> If it feels too easy, you're probably not learning.

Fluency feels like mastery but often isn't. The "illusion of comprehension" — where things feel familiar without being truly known — is one of the most common traps in self-study. Material that requires real effort to retrieve produces far better long-term retention than material that feels comfortable.

**Platform rule:** Distractors are deliberately plausible. Difficulty doesn't drop after a correct answer.

---

### 6. Interleaving
> Mix topics. Your brain will retain more.

Studying one topic in a block feels organized and productive. But interleaving multiple topics in a single session — even when it feels harder and more confusing — produces significantly better long-term retention. The confusion is the mechanism: the brain must identify which strategy applies to each problem, which strengthens recall.

**Platform rule:** Sessions draw from multiple topics, not a single unit.

---

### 7. Non-Interference
> Don't teach similar things back-to-back.

Closely related concepts interfere with each other's recall when learned in close succession. Spacing them apart in time — even just a few questions — dramatically reduces this interference. This is why traditional curricula that teach an entire unit of related material consecutively often produce worse retention than expected.

**Platform rule:** The scheduler avoids serving two closely-related topics consecutively.

---

### 8. Cognitive Load & Automaticity
> Automate the basics so the brain can handle harder things.

Working memory holds roughly 4 items at once. When foundational facts aren't automatic, they occupy slots needed for higher-order thinking. Automaticity — knowing something so thoroughly it requires no conscious effort — is what frees up working memory for harder problems.

**Platform rule:** Response time is tracked alongside accuracy. Slow-but-correct signals a topic needs more practice, not advancement.

---

### 9. Layering
> Learning advanced topics strengthens earlier ones.

When new material builds directly on prior knowledge, it reinforces that foundation — a process called retroactive facilitation. This is why a well-sequenced curriculum produces more durable knowledge than isolated topics: each new layer exercises and strengthens everything beneath it.

**Platform rule:** Topics are linked in a dependency graph. Advanced questions naturally exercise foundational concepts.

---

### 10. Targeted Remediation
> When a learner fails, find the specific gap and fill it.

Most failures trace to a specific missing sub-skill, not a complete lack of understanding. Broad re-teaching is inefficient. Identifying the exact gap — through which wrong answer was chosen — and drilling that specific prerequisite is far more effective.

**Platform rule:** Each wrong answer is tagged with the sub-skill it implicates. Failure triggers a targeted drill, not a topic reset.

---

## What NOT to do

| Mistake | Why it's wrong | Correct approach |
|---|---|---|
| Questions only at the end | Passive learning has minimal effect | Questions ARE the session |
| Advance after 1 correct answer | Illusion of comprehension | 3 consecutive OR 80% rolling |
| Block all new learning until reviews clear | Kills motivation | Interleave reviews with new topics |
| Same topic 3+ times in a row | Blocked practice → worse retention | Max 2 consecutive per topic |
| Serve identical topics back-to-back | Associative interference | Cluster-based scheduler gap |
| Generic "Wrong!" feedback | Misses the teachable moment | Distractor-specific explanation |
| Let learner skip failed topics | Bar-lowering | Remediate prerequisite, re-attempt |
| Never review mastered topics | Memory decays without review | SRS queue, served before new learning |
| Fixed answer choice order | Positional memorization, not learning | Shuffle on every render |
